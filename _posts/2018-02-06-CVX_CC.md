### Definition 

Let $f: \boldsymbol{R^n} \rightarrow \boldsymbol{R}$. The function $f^{\ast}: \boldsymbol{R^n} \rightarrow \boldsymbol{R}$, defined as

$$
\begin{eqnarray}
f^{\ast}(y) &=& \sup_{x \in \text{dom} f} \{y^Tx-f(x)\} \\
&=& \sup_{x \in \text{dom} f} \{\langle y, x \rangle - f(x)\} \quad \text{where,} \quad \langle y, x \rangle \quad  \text{denote the dot product}
\end{eqnarray}
$$
 
 is called the **conjugate** of the function $f$, where $$\sup$$ is the supremum The domain of the conjugate function consists of $y \in \boldsymbol{R^n}$ for which the *supremum* is finite. We see immediately [not for me] that $f^{\ast}$ is a convex function, since it is the pointwise supremum of a family of convex (indeed, affine) function of $y$. This is true whether or not f is convex.

 <img src="/images/posts/CVX/conjugate.png" height="370" width="600"> 

 $$
\begin{eqnarray}
\frac{\partial}{\partial x} \{y^Tx - f(x)\} &=& y - f'(x)\\
&=& 0 \\ 
\Rightarrow y &=& f'(x) \\
\Rightarrow x &=& (f')^{-1}(y)
\end{eqnarray}
$$

**Example 01:** *Affine function:* $f(x)=ax + b$ 

<img src="/images/posts/CVX/affine.png" height="480" width="640"> 

$$
\begin{eqnarray}
y &=& f'(x) \\
&=& \frac{\partial}{\partial x} \{ax + b\} \\
&=& a \\
\text{Therefore} \quad f^{\ast}(y) &=& \sup_{x \in \text{dom} f} \{y^Tx-f(x)\} \\
&=& \sup_{x \in \text{dom}} \{ y^T x - (ax + b)\} \\
&=& \{ a^2 -(a^2 + b) \} \\
f^{\ast}(a)&=& \{ - b \}
\end{eqnarray}
$$

Therefore the domain of the conjugate function $f^{\ast}$ is the singleton $$\{a\}$$, and $f^{\ast}(a) = -b$.

**Example 02:** *Negative logarithm:* $f(x)=-\log(x)$ with $\textbf{dom} f = \boldsymbol{R_{+}}$.

<img src="/images/posts/CVX/nlog.png" height="480" width="640"> 

$$
\begin{eqnarray}
y &=& f'(x) \\
&=& \frac{\partial}{\partial x} \{-\log(x)\} \\
&=& -\frac{1}{x} \\
\Rightarrow x &=& -\frac{1}{y} \\
\text{Therefore} \quad f^{\ast}(y) &=& \sup_{x \in \text{dom} f} \{y^Tx - (-\log(x))\} \\
&=& y (-\frac{1}{y}) - (-\log(-\frac{1}{y})) \\
&=& -\log(-y) - 1 \quad \text{for} \quad y < 0\\
\end{eqnarray}
$$

The function $$\{y^Tx - \log(-x)\}$$ is unbounded if $y \geq 0$, therefore, $$\textbf{dom} f^{\ast} = \{y \mid y < 0\} = -\boldsymbol{R_{++}}$$.

**Example 03:** *Exponential* $f(x)=e^x$

<img src="/images/posts/CVX/exp.png" height="480" width="640"> 

$$
\begin{eqnarray}
y &=& f'(x) \\
&=& \frac{\partial}{\partial x} \{e^x\} \\
&=& e^x \\
\Rightarrow x &=& \log(y) \\
\text{Therefore} \quad f^{\ast}(y) &=& \sup_{x \in \text{dom} f} \{y^Tx - (e^x)\} \\
&=& y \log(y) - (e^{\log(y)}) \\
&=& y \log(y) - y \quad \text{for} \quad y > 0 \\
\end{eqnarray}
$$

**Example 04:** *Negative Entropy* $f(x)=x\log(x)$ \# for nomalized probability

<img src="/images/posts/CVX/nentropy.png" height="480" width="640"> 

$$
\begin{eqnarray}
y &=& f'(x) \\
&=& \frac{\partial}{\partial x} \{x\log(x)\} \\
&=& \log(x) + 1 \\
\Rightarrow x &=& e^{y-1} \\
\text{Therefore} \quad f^{\ast}(y) &=& \sup_{x \in \text{dom} f} \{y^Tx - (x\log(x))\} \\
&=& y e^{y-1} - (e^{y-1} \log(e^{y-1})) \\
&=& y e^{y-1}- e^{y-1} (y-1) \\
&=&  e^{y-1} \quad \text{for} \quad \textbf{dom} f^{\ast} = \boldsymbol{R}\\
\end{eqnarray}
$$

**Example 05:** *Negative Entropy* $f(x)=x\log(x) - (x-1)$ \# for non-nomalized probability

<img src="/images/posts/CVX/nentropy_norm.png" height="480" width="640"> 

$$
\begin{eqnarray}
y &=& f'(x) \\
&=& \frac{\partial}{\partial x} \{x\log(x)-(x-1)\} \\
&=& \log(x)  \\
\Rightarrow x &=& e^{y} \\
\text{Therefore} \quad f^{\ast}(y) &=& \sup_{x \in \text{dom} f} \{y^Tx - (x\log(x)-(x-1))\} \\
&=& y e^{y} - (e^{y} \log(e^{y}) - (e^y - 1)) \\
&=& e^{y} - 1 \quad \text{for} \quad \textbf{dom} f^{\ast} = \boldsymbol{R}\\
\end{eqnarray}
$$


**Example 06:** *Inverse* $f(x)=\frac{1}{x}$

<img src="/images/posts/CVX/inverse.png" height="480" width="640"> 

$$
\begin{eqnarray}
y &=& f'(x) \\
&=& \frac{\partial}{\partial x} \{\frac{1}{x}\} \\
&=& - \frac{1}{x^2}\\
\Rightarrow x &=& \sqrt{-\frac{1}{y}} \\
\text{Therefore} \quad f^{\ast}(y) &=& \sup_{x \in \text{dom} f} \{y^Tx - (\frac{1}{x})\} \\
&=& y \sqrt{-\frac{1}{y}} - (\frac{1}{\sqrt{-\frac{1}{y}}}) \\
&=&  -2\sqrt{-y} \quad \text{for} \quad \textbf{dom} f^{\ast} = -\boldsymbol{R_{+}}\\
\end{eqnarray}
$$
