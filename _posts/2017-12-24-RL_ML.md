Maximum likelihood estimation $\text{(MLE)}$ is a method used for estimating the parameters of a given distribution, using obsered data. For example, we assume the policy of solving the CartPole problem is a gaussian distribution but the mean and variance are unknown. MLE can be used to estimate paramters, mean and variance, using a limited sample of the transitions $\text{(state, action, reward, next state)}$. But maximizing the total discounted reward, an optimal policy can be found, which comes with optimal parameters.

### Fromal Definition
Let $x_1, x_2, x_3, ..., x_n$ be observations from n independent and identically distributed random variables drawn from a probability distribution $f_0$, where $f_0$ is known to be from a family of distributions $f$ that formulated by paramters $\theta$, e.g., normal distribution $f(x|\theta) = f(x|\mu, \sigma) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2 \pi \sigma^2}} e^{-\frac{(x-\mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}}$.

The goal of **Maximum likelihood estimation** is to maximize the likelihood function:

$$
\begin{eqnarray}
\max L(\theta) = f(x_1, x_2, x_3, ..., x_n|\theta) = f(x_1|\theta) \times f(x_2|\theta) \times f(x_3|\theta) ..., \times f(x_n|\theta) 
\end{eqnarray}
$$

Often, the **averaged log-likelihood** function is easier to work with:

$$
\begin{eqnarray}
\hat{\ell} = \frac{1}{n} \log L = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} \log f(x_i | \theta)
\end{eqnarray}
$$

I don't quite understand the definition of **Weighted Maximum Likilihood Eastination**, but still it can be formulated as,

$$
\begin{eqnarray}
\max L(\theta) = \max \sum_{i} \omega^{[i]} \log f(x_i | \theta) = \max \sum_{i} \omega^{[i]} \log f(x_i | \mu, \Sigma)
\end{eqnarray}
$$

### Gaussian Policy

For example, for a gaussian policy,

$$
\begin{eqnarray}
\pi(\boldsymbol{\theta}; \boldsymbol{\omega}) = \mathcal{N}(\boldsymbol{\theta}| \boldsymbol{\mu}, \boldsymbol{\Sigma} )
\end{eqnarray}
$$

Derive analytically the update rule for the policy.

$$
\begin{eqnarray}
\boldsymbol{\omega_{k+1}} = \underset{\boldsymbol{\omega}}{\mathrm{argmax}} \{\sum_{i} w^{[i]} \log \pi (\boldsymbol{\theta}^{[i]}; \boldsymbol{\omega}) \}
\end{eqnarray}
$$

**for the mean $\mu$** 

$$
\begin{eqnarray}
\nabla_{\boldsymbol{\mu}} \{\sum_{i} w^{[i]} \log \pi (\boldsymbol{\theta}^{[i]}; \boldsymbol{\omega}) \}  
&=&   \sum_{i} w^{[i]} \nabla_{\boldsymbol{\mu}} \log \pi (\boldsymbol{\theta}^{[i]}; \boldsymbol{\omega}) \\ 
&=& \sum_{i} w^{[i]} \nabla_{\boldsymbol{\mu}} \log \frac{1}{\boldsymbol{\Sigma}\sqrt{2\pi}} \exp (\frac{(\boldsymbol{\theta^{[i]} - \mu})^2}{2\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^2}) \\
&=& \sum_{i} w^{[i]} \nabla_{\boldsymbol{\mu}} \{\log(1) - \log(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}) - \log(\sqrt{2\pi}) + \log( \exp (\frac{(\boldsymbol{\theta^{[i]} - \mu})^2}{2\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^2})) \}\\
&=& \sum_{i} w^{[i]} \{ 0 - 0 - 0 + \nabla_{\boldsymbol{\mu}} \log( \exp (\frac{(\boldsymbol{\theta^{[i]} - \mu})^2}{2\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^2})) \} \\
&=& \sum_{i} w^{[i]} \nabla_{\boldsymbol{\mu}}  \frac{(\boldsymbol{\theta^{[i]} - \mu})^2}{2\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^2} \\
&=& \sum_{i} w^{[i]} \frac{(\boldsymbol{\theta^{[i]} - \mu})}{\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^2} \\
&=& 0
\end{eqnarray}
$$

$$
\begin{eqnarray}
\Longrightarrow \boldsymbol{\mu} = \frac{\sum_i \omega^{[i]} \boldsymbol{\theta}^{[i]}}{\sum_i \omega^{[i]}}
\end{eqnarray}
$$

**for the variance $\Sigma$**

$$
\begin{eqnarray}
\Longrightarrow \boldsymbol{\Sigma} = \frac{\sum_i \omega^{[i]} (\boldsymbol{\theta}^{[i]} - \boldsymbol{\mu})(\boldsymbol{\theta}^{[i]} - \boldsymbol{\mu})^T}{\sum_i \omega^{[i]}}
\end{eqnarray}
$$

**difference to policy gradients**

> * ***weighted maximum likelihood***
    * $\boldsymbol{\omega_{k+1}} = \underset{\boldsymbol{\omega}}{\mathrm{argmax}} \{\sum_{i} w^{[i]} \log \pi (\boldsymbol{\theta}^{[i]}; \boldsymbol{\omega}) \}$
    * $\nabla_{\boldsymbol{\mu}} \{\sum_{i} w^{[i]} \log \pi (\boldsymbol{\theta}^{[i]}; \boldsymbol{\omega}) \}  
       = \sum_{i} w^{[i]} \nabla_{\boldsymbol{\mu}} \log \pi (\boldsymbol{\theta}^{[i]}; \boldsymbol{\omega}) 
       = 0$
    * solve in closed form for $\boldsymbol{\omega}$ 
    * no learning rate
    * weights $\omega^{[i]}=f(R^{[i]})$
* ***policy gradients***
    * $\boldsymbol{\omega_{k+1}}= \boldsymbol{\omega_{k}} + \nabla_{\boldsymbol{\omega}} J_{\boldsymbol{\omega}}$
    * $\nabla_{\boldsymbol{\omega}} J_{\boldsymbol{\omega}} = \sum_{i} R_i \nabla_{\boldsymbol{\omega}} \log \pi (\boldsymbol{\theta}^{[i]}; \boldsymbol{\omega})$
    * solve taking the gradients
    * learning rate $\alpha$


### 2-DoF plannar robot (this environment was originally developed by <a target="_blank" href="http://www.ias.tu-darmstadt.de/">  Intelligent Autonomous System Group </a> at TU Darmstadt)

The goal is to control a 2-Dof plannar robot to throw a ball at a specific target. We use an episodic setup, where we first specify the parameters of the policy, evaluate them on the simulated system, and obtain a reward. The robot will be controlled with the Dynamic Motor Primitives $\text{\(DMPs\)}$. The goal state of the DMPs is pre-specified and the weights of the DMP $\theta_i, i = 1 ... 10$ are the open parameters of the control policy. Each DoF of the robot is controlled with a DMP with five basis functions. The ball is mounted at the end-effecor of the robot and gets automatically released at time step $t_{rel}$. We define a stochastic distribution $\pi(\boldsymbol{\theta \mid \omega}) = \mathcal{N}(\boldsymbol{\theta} \mid \boldsymbol{\mu}, \boldsymbol{\Sigma})$, where an upper-level policy is defined as $\pi_{\boldsymbol{\omega}}(\boldsymbol{\theta})$. The upper-level policy chooses the parameters $\boldsymbol{\theta}$ of the lower-level policy $\pi_{\boldsymbol{\theta}}(\boldsymbol{\mu \mid x})$ that executed on the robot.

**1. Dynamic Motor Primitives $\text{\(DMPs\)}$**

Dynamic Motor Primitives $\text{\(DMPs\)}$ are the most widely used time-dependent policy representation in robotics. DMPs use nonlinear dynamical systems for generating the movement of the robot. The key principle of DMPs is to a use in linear sping-damper system which is modulated by a nonlinear forcing function $f_{\theta}$. 

$$
\begin{eqnarray}
\ddot{y} &=& \tau^2(\alpha (\beta(g-y) - (\dot{y}/\tau)) + f_\theta(z)) \\
\dot{z} &=& -\tau \alpha_z z
\end{eqnarray}
$$

where $y$ is the state of the system, $\dot{y}$ and $\ddot{y}$ are the first and second time derivatives, respectively. The attractor's goal is denoted by $g$ and the forcing function by $f_\omega$. The parameters $\alpha$ and $\beta$ control the sping-damper system. The phase variable is denoted by $z$ and the **temporal scaling coefficient** by $\tau$.

The forcing function $f_{\theta}$ is given by,

$$
\begin{eqnarray}
\boldsymbol{ f_{\theta}(z) = \frac{\sum_{i=0}^{K} \phi_i(z) \theta_i z}{\sum_{j=0}^{K} \phi_j(z)}=\psi(z)^T \theta} \quad \text{with} \quad \boldsymbol{\psi_i(z) = \frac{\phi_i(z) z}{\sum_{j=1}^{K} \phi_j (z)}}
\end{eqnarray}
$$

where the basis functions $\phi_i(z)$ are Gaussian basis given by,

$$
\begin{eqnarray}
\phi_i(z) = \exp(-0.5 (z-c_i)^2 / h_i)
\end{eqnarray}
$$

where the centers $c$ are equally distributed in the phase $z$, and the width $h$ is an open parameter.

class Pend2dBallThrowDMP(object):
    def __init__(self):
        self.numBasis = 5
        self.numTrajSteps = 100
        self.dimJoints = 2
        self.dt = 0.01
        self.lengths = np.ones(self.dimJoints)
        self.masses = np.ones(self.dimJoints)
        self.initState = np.array([-pi, 0., 0., 0.])
        self.ballGoal = [2., 1.]
        self.releaseStep = 50
        self.Kp = 1400.

    def getDesiredTrajDMP(self, dmp_w):
        alphaz = 8./3.
        alpha  = 25.
        beta   = alpha/4.
        tau    = 1.
        Ts     = 1.
        g      = self.initState[::2]
        
        #
        
        C = np.exp(-alphaz*np.arange(self.numBasis)/(self.numBasis-1)*Ts)
        H = 0.5 / (0.65 * np.diff(C)**2)
        H = np.append(H,H[-1])
        
        #

        q = np.zeros((self.numTrajSteps, 2*self.dimJoints))
        q[0,:] = self.initState
        x = np.ones(self.numTrajSteps)

        #

        for i in range(self.numTrajSteps-1):
            psi = np.exp(-H*(x[i]-C)**2)
            f = np.dot(dmp_w.T, psi) * x[i] / np.sum(psi)
            qdd_des = (alpha * (beta * ( g - q[i,::2] ) - ( q[i,1::2] / tau ) ) + f.T ) * tau**2
            q[i+1,1::2] = q[i,1::2] + qdd_des * self.dt
            q[i+1,::2] = q[i,::2] + q[i+1,1::2] * self.dt
            xd = -alphaz*x[i]*tau
            x[i+1] = x[i] + xd * self.dt
        return q

**2. Soluntions for Gaussian Policy**

For a Gaussian Policy : 

$$
\begin{eqnarray}
\pi(\boldsymbol{\theta; \omega}) = \mathcal{N}(\boldsymbol{\theta} \mid \boldsymbol{\mu}, \boldsymbol{\Sigma} )
\end{eqnarray}
$$

using the weighted Maximum Likelihood estimat,

$$
\begin{eqnarray}
\boldsymbol{\omega_{k+1}} = \underset{\boldsymbol{\omega}}{\mathrm{argmax}} \{\sum_{i} w^{[i]} \log \pi (\boldsymbol{\theta}^{[i]}; \boldsymbol{\omega}) \}
\end{eqnarray}
$$

the updata rules for mean $\mu$ and covariance $\Sigma$ are,

$$
\begin{eqnarray}
\boldsymbol{\mu} &=& \frac{\sum_i \omega^{[i]} \boldsymbol{\theta}^{[i]}}{\sum_i \omega^{[i]}} \\
\boldsymbol{\Sigma} &=& \frac{\sum_i \omega^{[i]} (\boldsymbol{\theta}^{[i]} - \boldsymbol{\mu})(\boldsymbol{\theta}^{[i]} - \boldsymbol{\mu})^T}{\sum_i \omega^{[i]}}
\end{eqnarray}
$$

to calculate the weights $\boldsymbol{\omega_i}$ for each sample, transform the returned rewards by, 

$$
\begin{eqnarray}
\boldsymbol{\omega^{[i]}} = \exp((\boldsymbol{R^{[i]}(\theta)} - \max(\boldsymbol{R})) \beta)
\end{eqnarray}
$$

where the vector $\boldsymbol{R} \in \mathbb{R}^{Nx1}$ is constructed from the rewards of the $N$ samples. The parameter $\beta$ is set to

$$
\begin{eqnarray}
\beta = \frac{\lambda}{\max(\boldsymbol{R}) - \min(\boldsymbol{R})} \quad \text{where} \quad \lambda \quad \text{is called temperature} 
\end{eqnarray}
$$

In theory, the choosing of the temperature $\lambda$ falls under a more general Reinforcement Learning problem, exploration-exploitation problem. A larger value $\lambda$ would direct the algorithm to areas with higher reward faster.
However, problems may raise as the algorithm tends to get stuck to a specific solution $\text{(local optima)}$ instead of exploring for better paramters of policy. A smaller $\lambda$ is more likely to find better optimal parameters for the policy, but the converge speed can be considered too slow. 

**3. Exploration-exploitation problem**

On of the biggest challenge in reinforcement learning is the choice of explorative behavior of the agent: when should it exploit the policy and move towards state that it knows are good, and when should it explore the environment more searching for better rewards. 

**Relative Entropy Policy Search $\text{(REPS)}$** is a policy search method that aims at finding the optimal policy that maximizes the expected return. At the same time, we bound the exploration and exploitation by using relative entropy between the observed data distribution $q(s,a)$ and the data distribution $p^{\pi}(s,a)$ generated by the new policy $\pi$. The parameters are learned such that the agent goes towards the best states experienced so far, but at the same time the bound avoids ”too steep“ updates and keeps the policy explorative. However, the KL divergence bound has to be set manually.
