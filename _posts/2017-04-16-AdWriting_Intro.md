### Introduction
8 March 2017, one day after I send my first robot learning report draft to my supervisor Boris Belousov, Boris recommended this book to me, *The Elements of Style* by E. B. White and William I. Strunk. From my perspective, I found this book quite useful, because the authors are not trying to teach you English grammar, instead, they tell you what is the right way of expressing your opinions.

**A little bit history about this book (from wikipedia)**: ***The Eleements of Style*** is a prescriptive American English writing style guide in numerous editions. The original was composed by William Strunk Jr., in 1918, and published by Harcourt, in 1920, comprising eight "elementary rules of usage", ten "elementary principles of composition", "a few matters of form", a list of 49 "words and expressions commonly misused", and a list of 57 "words of misspelled". E. B. White greatly enlarged and revised this book for publication by Macmillan in 1959. That was the first edition of the so-called **"Strunk & White"**, which Time magazine named in 2011 as one of the 100 best and most influential books written in English since 1923.    

### I  *Elementary Rules of Usage*  (**Possessive**)

**1. Form the possessive singular of nouns by adding 's**

***(1) Basic Rules***

When we want to show that something belongs to somebody or something, we usually add an apostrophe + s ('s) to a singular noun and an apostrophe (') to a plural noun, for example:

> * Basic rules
	* the boy's ball (singlura noun)
	* the boys' ball (plural noun)
	* Charles's friend (Proper Nouns 专有名词)
	* Burns's poems (Proper Nouns 专有名词)
	* the witch's malice (Proper Nouns 专有名词)
* Exceptions （1） the possessives of ancient proper names ending in ***-es*** and ***-is***
	* e.g. Jesus' father
* Exceptions（2） such forms as:
	* for conscience' sake
	* for righteousness' sake
* Exceptions (3) 
	* Moses' Laws vs. the laws of Moses (better)
	* Isis' temple vs. the temple of Isis (better)

***(2) Pronominal Possessives***

The pronominal possessives hers, its, theirs, yours, and ours have no apostrophe (单引号／撇号). Indefinite pronouns, however, use the apostrophe to show possession. 

> * one's rights
* somebody eles's umbrella

***(3) Irregular Plurals***

Some nouns have irregular plural forms without s (man - men). To show possession, we usually add 's to the plural form of these nouns:

> * my child's dog vs. my children's dog
* the man's work vs. the men's work
* the mouse's cage vs. the mice's cage
* a person's clothes vs. people's clothes
