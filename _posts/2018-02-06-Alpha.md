The **$\alpha$-divergence** {(Amari, 1985)} or the **Renyi divergence** of order $\alpha$ measures the "similarity" between two distributions, we adopt a more convenient form {(Zhu, 1995)} in this blog, the $\alpha$-divergence of a distribution $P$ from a distribution $Q$ is defined as

$$
\begin{eqnarray}
D_{\alpha} (P||Q) = \frac{1}{\alpha-1} \log{(\sum_{i=1}^{n} \frac{p_i^{\alpha}}{q_i^{\alpha-1}})}
\end{eqnarray}
$$

The **$f$-divergence** {(Csiszar, 1963)} generalizes many similarity measures between probability distributions. Let $P$ and $Q$ be two probability distributions over a space $\Omega$ such that $P$ is absolutely continuous with respect the $Q$. Then for a convex function $f$ such that $f(1)=0$, the $f$-divergence of $P$ from $Q$ is defined as 

$$
\begin{eqnarray}
D_f (P||Q) = \int_{\Omega} f(\frac{dP}{dQ}) dQ
\end{eqnarray}
$$

### Renyi entropy
Before we dive deep into the $\alpha$-divergence, let's take a look at a more general concept, the **Renyi Entropy**. In information theory, the **Renyi Entropy** generalizes the *Hartley entropy*, the *Shannon entropy*, the *Collision entropy* and the *Min entropy*. The Renyi entropy of order $\alpha$, where $\alpha \geq 0$ and $\alpha \neq 0$, is defined formlly as, 

$$
\begin{eqnarray}
H_{\alpha}(X) = \frac{1}{1-\alpha} \log (\sum_{i=1}^n p_i^{\alpha})
\end{eqnarray}
$$

Here, X is a discrete random variable with possible outcomes $1,2,3...,n$ and corresponding possibilities $p_i=P(X=i)$ for $i=1,..., n$, and the logarithm is base 2.

> * Hartley entropy : $$H_0(X) = \log(n)$$
* Shannon entropy : $$H_1(X)=-\sum_{i=1}^{n} p_i \log(p_i)$$
* Collision entropy : $$H_2(X) = -\log{\sum_{i=1}^n p_i^2}$$
* Min entropy :  $$H_\infty(X) = \min_i (-\log p_i)$$

### $f$-divergence 

The **$f$-divergence** {(Csiszar, 1963)} generalizes many similarity measures between probability distributions. Let $P$ and $Q$ be two probability distributions over a space $\Omega$ such that $P$ is absolutely continuous with respect the $Q$. Then for a convex function $f$ such that $f(1)=0$, the $f$-divergence of $P$ from $Q$ is defined as 

$$
\begin{eqnarray}
D_f (P||Q) = \int_{\Omega} f(\frac{dP}{dQ}) dQ
\end{eqnarray}
$$

where $$\frac{dP}{dQ}$$ denotes the Radon-Nikodym derivative {or density} of $P$ with respect to $Q$

If $P$ and $Q$ are both absolutely continuous with respect to a reference distribution $\mu$ on $\Omega$ then their probability densities $p$ and $q$ satidify $dP=p d \mu$ and $dQ=qd\mu$. In this case, the $f$-divergence can be written as,

$$
\begin{eqnarray}
D_f (P||Q) = \int_{\Omega} q(x)  f(\frac{p(x)}{q(x)}) d \mu (x)
\end{eqnarray}
$$

where the **generator function $f$ is a convex function on $$(0, \infty)$$, and lower-semicontinous function satisfying $f(1)=0$.**

Here is a list of generator functions together with function's derivative. 

$$
\begin{eqnarray}
&:& \quad \quad \quad \quad f(x) &:& \quad \quad \quad \quad f'(x)& \\
\text{========} &:& \text{==============} &:& \text{===========} \\
\text{KL} \quad &:& \quad  \quad x\log(x) - (x-1) &:& \quad \quad \log(x)\\
\text{Reverse KL} \quad &:&  \quad \quad -\log(x)+(x+1) &:& \quad \quad -\frac{1}{x} + 1\\
\text{Pearson} \chi^2 \quad &:&  \quad \quad \frac{1}{2} (x-1)^2 &:& \quad \quad x-1\\
\text{Neyman} \chi^2 \quad &:& \quad  \quad \frac{(x-1)^2}{2x} &:& \quad \quad -\frac{1}{2x^2} + \frac{1}{2}\\
\text{Hellinger} \quad &:& \quad  \quad 2(\sqrt{x}-1)^2 &:& \quad \quad 2 - \frac{2}{\sqrt{x}}\\
\end{eqnarray}
$$


$$
\begin{eqnarray}
\text{Jensen-Shannon} \quad &:& \quad  \quad x\log(x)-(1+x)\log(\frac{1+x}{2}) &:& \quad \quad -\log{\frac{1+x}{2x}} - 1\\
\text{GAN} \quad &:& \quad  \quad x\log(x)-(1+x)\log(x+1) &:& \quad \quad - \log{\frac{1+x}{x}}\\
\end{eqnarray}
$$

### $\alpha$-divergence [Renyi divergence]

Again, the **$\alpha$-divergence** {(Amari, 1985)} or the **Renyi divergence** of order $\alpha$ measures the "similarity" between two distributions, we adopt a more convenient form {(Zhu, 1995)} in this blog, the $\alpha$-divergence of a distribution $P$ from a distribution $Q$ is defined as

$$
\begin{eqnarray}
D_{\alpha} (P||Q) &=& \frac{1}{\alpha-1} \log{(\sum_{i=1}^{n} \frac{p_i^{\alpha}}{q_i^{\alpha-1}})} \quad \text{from wikipedia}\\
\text{or} \quad D_{\alpha} (P||Q) &=& \frac{1}{1-\alpha} (1 - \int \frac{p_i^{\alpha}}{q_i^{\alpha-1}}) \quad \text{from Zhu, 1995}
\end{eqnarray}
$$

The **$\alpha$-divergence** can also defined as a one-parameter family of $f$-divergences generated by the $\alpha$-function with $\alpha \in \mathbb{R}$.
