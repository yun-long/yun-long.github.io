### numpy.dot [a,b, out=None]
***Dot product of two arrays.***

> * For 2-D arrays it is equivalent to matrix multiplication.
* For 1-D arrays to inner product of vectors [without complex conjugation]. 
* For N dimensions it is a sum product over the last axis of a and the second-to-last of b

2-D array
{% highlight python %}
>>> a = np.array([[1,2,3], \
                  [4,5,6]])    # shape = (2,3)

>>> b = np.array([[2,3],   \
                  [3,4],   \
                  [8,9]])      # shape = (3,2)
>>> np.dot(a,b)
array([[10, 13],
       [16, 21]])
{% endhighlight %}

1-D array
{% highlight python %}
>>> a = np.array([1,2,3])
>>> b = np.array([2,3,4])
>>> np.dot(a,b)
20
>>> np.matmul(a,b)
20
{% endhighlight %}

### numpy.matmul [x1, x2, out=None]

***Matrix product of two arrays.***

* If both arguments are 2-D they are multiplied like conventional matrices.
{% highlight python %}
>>> a = np.array([[1,2,3], \
                  [4,5,6]])    # shape = (2,3)

>>> b = np.array([[2,3],   \
                  [3,4],   \
                  [8,9]])      # shape = (3,2)
>>> np.matmul(a,b)
array([[10, 13],
       [16, 21]])
{% endhighlight %}
* If the first argument is 1-D, it is promoted to a matrix by prepending a 1 to its dimensions. After matrix multiplication the prepended 1 is removed. e.g.:
$$
\left[ \begin{array}{c} 1 \\ 2 \end{array} \right] \times \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 9 & 2 \end{bmatrix} 
= 
\begin{bmatrix} 1 \times 1 + 2 \times 9  & 1 \times 0 + 2 \times 2 \end{bmatrix} 
= \left[ \begin{array}{c} 19&4\end{array} \right] 
$$
* If the second argument is 1-D, it is promoted to a matrix by appending a 1 to its dimensions. After matrix multiplication the appended 1 is removed. e.g.:
$$
\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 9 & 2 \end{bmatrix} \times 
\left[ \begin{array}{c} 1 \\ 2 \end{array} \right] = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 9 & 2 \end{bmatrix} \times 
\left[ \begin{array}{c} 1 & 0 \\ 2 & 0 \end{array} \right] = 
\begin{bmatrix} 1 \times 1 + 0 \times 2 + 1 \times 0 + 0 \times 0  \\ 9 \times 1 + 2 \times 2 + 9 \times 0 + 2 \times 0 \end{bmatrix} = \left[ \begin{array}{c} 1 \\ 13 \end{array} \right] 
$$
{% highlight python %}
>>> x1 = np.array([[1,0], \
                   [9,2]])
>>> x2 = np.array([1,2])
>>> np.matmul(x1,x2)
[ 1 13]
>>> np.matmul(x2,x1)
[19  4]
{% endhighlight %}
* If either argument is N-D, N > 2, it is treated as a stack of matrices residing in the last two indexes and broadcast accordingly.

*matmul* differs from *dot* in two important ways:

> * Multiplication by scalars is not allowed.
* Stacks of matrices are broadcast together as if the matrices were elements.
